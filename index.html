Epithelial Tissue

Definition:
Tissue that covers body surfaces, lines body cavities, and forms glands. Characterized by tightly packed cells with little extracellular matrix.

A. Characteristics

  • Cellularity: Composed almost entirely of cells
  • Polarity: Has apical (free) and basal (attached) surfaces
  • Attachment: Basal surface attaches to basement membrane
  • Avascularity: No blood vessels
  • Regeneration: High capacity for renewal

B. Classification

Based on two factors:

  1. Cell Shape: Squamous (flat), Cuboidal (cube), Columnar (tall)
  2. Number of Layers: Simple (single), Stratified (multiple)

C. Types of Epithelia

Simple Epithelia (Single Layer)

Simple Squamous

  • Structure: Thin, flat cells
  • Locations:
    • Mesothelium - lines body cavities (peritoneal, pleural, pericardial)
    • Endothelium - lines heart and blood vessels
  • Function: Allows diffusion/filtration

Simple Cuboidal

  • Structure: Cube-shaped cells
  • Locations: Kidney tubules, gland ducts
  • Function: Secretion and absorption

Simple Columnar

  • Structure: Tall cells, may have microvilli
  • Locations: Stomach, intestine, gallbladder
  • Function: Protection, secretion, absorption

Stratified Epithelia (Multiple Layers)

Stratified Squamous

  • Structure: Multiple layers, surface cells flat
  • Types:
    • Keratinized: Skin (dead surface cells filled with keratin)
    • Non-keratinized: Mouth, throat, esophagus, vagina
  • Function: Protection against abrasion

Stratified Cuboidal

  • Structure: Rare, 2-3 layers of cube-shaped cells
  • Locations: Sweat gland ducts
  • Function: Protection, secretion, absorption

Special Types

Pseudostratified Columnar

  • Structure: All cells touch basement membrane but appear layered
  • Locations: Trachea, bronchi, male reproductive tract
  • Function: Protection, secretion, moves mucus with cilia

Transitional Epithelium (Urothelium)

  • Structure: Cells change from cuboidal to squamous when stretched
  • Locations: Urinary bladder, ureters, renal pelvis
  • Function: Permits stretching without damage

D. Glandular Epithelia

Exocrine Glands (secrete onto surfaces via ducts)

Modes of Secretion:

  1. Merocrine: Secretion by exocytosis (e.g., salivary glands)
  2. Apocrine: Lose apical cytoplasm (e.g., mammary glands)
  3. Holocrine: Whole cell bursts (e.g., sebaceous glands)

Structural Types:

  • Simple: Duct does not branch (intestinal glands, sweat glands)
  • Compound: Duct branches (salivary glands, mammary glands)
  • Unicellular: Single cells (goblet cells in intestine)

Connective Tissue

Definition:
The most abundant tissue type that binds together, supports, and strengthens other body tissues. Characterized by cells dispersed in an extracellular matrix.

A. General Characteristics

  • Most abundant tissue type
  • Components: Cells + Extracellular Matrix (Fibers + Ground Substance)
  • Functions: Binding, support, protection, insulation, transport, defense

B. Cells of Connective Tissue Proper

Cell Type Function
Fibroblasts Most common; produce fibers and ground substance
Fibrocytes Maintain connective tissue fibers
Adipocytes Fat cells; store lipids
Mesenchymal Cells Stem cells for repair
Macrophages Phagocytic; engulf pathogens/debris
Mast Cells Release histamine (inflammation) and heparin (anticoagulant)
Lymphocytes Immune cells
Microphages Phagocytic blood cells (neutrophils, eosinophils)

C. Classification of Connective Tissues

1. Connective Tissue Proper

Loose Connective Tissues:

Areolar Tissue
  • Structure: Loose arrangement of all fibers and cells
  • Locations: Under epithelia, around organs, between muscles
  • Function: Cushions, permits movement
Adipose Tissue
  • Structure: Dominated by adipocytes (fat cells)
  • Locations: Subcutaneous fat, around kidneys/eyes, breasts
  • Function: Padding, insulation, energy storage
Reticular Tissue
  • Structure: Network of reticular fibers
  • Locations: Liver, spleen, lymph nodes, bone marrow
  • Function: Forms supportive stroma (framework)

Dense Connective Tissues:

Dense Regular
  • Structure: Parallel collagen fibers
  • Locations: Tendons (muscle to bone), Ligaments (bone to bone)
  • Function: Resists tension in one direction
Dense Irregular
  • Structure: Irregular collagen fibers
  • Locations: Dermis of skin, organ capsules
  • Function: Resists tension from multiple directions
Elastic Tissue
  • Structure: Dominated by elastic fibers
  • Locations: Large arteries (aorta), elastic ligaments, erectile tissue
  • Function: Allows stretch and recoil

2. Fluid Connective Tissues

Blood:

  • Matrix: Plasma (liquid)
  • Cells:
    • Erythrocytes (RBCs): Transport O₂/CO₂; no nucleus
    • Leukocytes (WBCs): Defense (monocytes, lymphocytes, eosinophils, neutrophils, basophils)
    • Platelets: Cell fragments for clotting

Lymph:

  • Derived from interstitial fluid
  • Circulates in lymphatic vessels
  • Contains lymphocytes for immune monitoring
  • Returns to veins near heart

3. Supporting Connective Tissues

Cartilage:

Avascular; cells = chondrocytes in lacunae

Hyaline Cartilage
  • Structure: Most common; tough but flexible
  • Locations: Rib-sternum joints, nasal cartilage, trachea, fetal skeleton
  • Function: Support, reduces friction
Elastic Cartilage
  • Structure: Many elastic fibers; very flexible
  • Locations: External ear, epiglottis
  • Function: Support with flexibility
Fibrocartilage
  • Structure: Dense collagen fibers; very tough
  • Locations: Intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis, knee menisci
  • Function: Resists compression, absorbs shock

Cartilage Growth:

  1. Interstitial: From within (chondrocytes divide)
  2. Appositional: At surface (chondroblasts in perichondrium)

Bone (Osseous Tissue):

  • Structure: Calcified matrix with calcium salts
  • Cells: Osteocytes in lacunae
  • Organization: Osteons with central canals
  • Nutrient Transport: Canaliculi (tiny canals)
  • Covering: Periosteum (fibrous + cellular layers)

D. Fascia: Layers of Connective Tissue

  1. Superficial Fascia (Subcutaneous layer/Hypodermis):
    • Areolar + adipose tissue
    • Separates skin from deeper structures
    • Provides insulation and padding
  2. Deep Fascia:
    • Dense regular connective tissue
    • Forms strong fibrous framework
    • Binds muscles together
  3. Subserous Fascia:
    • Areolar tissue
    • Between deep fascia and serous membranes

Muscle Tissue

Definition:
Tissue specialized for contraction to produce movement. Three types with different structures and functions.

Key Concept

All muscle tissues are excitable (respond to stimuli), contractile (can shorten), extensible (can stretch), and elastic (return to original length).

Type Structure Location Function Control
Skeletal Muscle Long, cylindrical, striated, multinucleated Attached to bones Voluntary movement; generates heat; protects organs Voluntary
Cardiac Muscle Short, branched, striated, 1–2 nuclei; intercalated discs Heart wall (myocardium) Pumps blood; involuntary contraction Involuntary
Smooth Muscle Short, spindle-shaped, non-striated, 1 nucleus Walls of hollow organs (gut, blood vessels, bladder) Moves materials; regulates organ diameter Involuntary

Exam Tip

Remember: Skeletal = voluntary, striated, multinucleated. Cardiac = involuntary, striated, intercalated discs. Smooth = involuntary, non-striated, spindle-shaped.

Membranes

Definition:
Thin layers of epithelial and connective tissue that line body surfaces, cavities, and organs.
Membrane Type Structure Location Function
Mucous Membrane (Mucosa) Epithelium + Lamina Propria (areolar tissue) Lines cavities open to exterior (digestive, respiratory, urinary, reproductive tracts) Protection, secretion, absorption; kept moist
Serous Membrane (Serosa) Mesothelium (simple squamous) + areolar tissue Lines sealed internal cavities Reduces friction; secretes serous fluid
Cutaneous Membrane Stratified squamous epithelium + dense irregular connective tissue The skin Protection, sensation, thermoregulation
Synovial Membrane Areolar tissue + adipose; no epithelium Lines joint cavities Secretes synovial fluid; reduces friction in joints

Serous Membrane Types

  1. Peritoneum: Abdominal cavity
  2. Pleura: Lungs
  3. Pericardium: Heart

Key Concept

All membranes consist of an epithelial layer and an underlying connective tissue layer. The type of epithelium and connective tissue varies based on the membrane's location and function.

Integumentary System (Skin)

Definition:
The body's largest organ system consisting of skin and its accessory structures (hair, nails, glands).

A. Structure

Layers:

  1. Epidermis: Stratified squamous epithelium (keratinized)
    • Layers: Stratum corneum, lucidum, granulosum, spinosum, basale
  2. Dermis: Dense irregular connective tissue
    • Papillary layer: Areolar tissue
    • Reticular layer: Dense irregular CT
    • Contains: Blood vessels, glands, hair follicles
  3. Hypodermis (Subcutaneous): Adipose + areolar tissue
    • Not part of skin proper

B. Accessory Structures

  • Hair
  • Nails
  • Sweat glands
  • Sebaceous (oil) glands

C. Functions

  1. Protection: Physical barrier against pathogens/environment
  2. Temperature Regulation: Sweat glands, blood vessel adjustment
  3. Sensation: Contains sensory receptors
  4. Vitamin D Synthesis: Sunlight exposure initiates production
  5. Excretion: Through sweat
  6. Absorption: Limited (e.g., medications)

D. Sensory Receptors in Skin

Free Nerve Endings:

  • Nociceptors: Detect pain
  • Thermoreceptors: Detect temperature
  • Others: Touch, itch

Encapsulated Nerve Endings:

  • Meissner's Corpuscles: Light touch, pressure
  • Pacinian Corpuscles: Deep pressure, vibration
  • Ruffini Endings: Deep pressure, stretch
  • Merkel Cells: Fine touch, texture

Hair Follicle Receptors:

  • Wrap around hair follicles
  • Sense hair movement

E. Skin Types

  • Thick Skin: Hairless (palms, soles); more stratum corneum layers
  • Thin Skin: Hairy (most of body); fewer layers

Exam Tip

Remember the order of epidermal layers from deep to superficial: Stratum Basale, Spinosum, Granulosum, Lucidum (only in thick skin), Corneum. Use mnemonic: "British Soldiers Get Lots of Cookies"

Key Exam Concepts

Essential Knowledge for SFU Exam

Focus on these high-yield topics for your physiology exam:

  1. Epithelial Classification: Know all types with locations and functions
    • Simple vs. stratified
    • Squamous, cuboidal, columnar shapes
    • Special types: pseudostratified, transitional
  2. Connective Tissue Cells: Functions of key cells
    • Fibroblasts, fibrocytes, adipocytes
    • Macrophages, mast cells (histamine/heparin)
    • Mesenchymal cells (stem cells)
  3. Dense Regular vs. Irregular:
    • Regular: Parallel fibers → tendons/ligaments
    • Irregular: Irregular fibers → dermis/organ capsules
  4. Cartilage Types: Locations and special properties
    • Hyaline: Most common, reduces friction
    • Elastic: Flexible (ear, epiglottis)
    • Fibrocartilage: Tough (intervertebral discs)
  5. Membranes: Structure and locations
    • Mucous, serous, cutaneous, synovial
    • Know epithelial type and connective tissue component
  6. Skin Layers: Epidermis, dermis, hypodermis
    • Components and functions of each
    • Epidermal layers in order
  7. Sensory Receptors: Types and what they detect
    • Free nerve endings vs. encapsulated
    • Specific receptors: Meissner's, Pacinian, Ruffini, Merkel
  8. Fascia Layers: Superficial, deep, subserous
    • Composition and function of each

Final Study Strategy

Focus on structure-function relationships. For each tissue type, ask: "Why is it structured this way for its location and function?" This approach will help you understand rather than just memorize, leading to better exam performance.
